Acts 3:19 says, “Repent ye therefore, and be converted, that your sins may be blotted out, when the times of refreshing shall come from the presence of the Lord.” This verse highlights two distinct actions: repentance and conversion, and in the Greek text, these are represented by different words.

1. Repentance (metanoeó - Greek: μετανοέω)

The word “repent” in this verse is translated from the Greek word metanoeó, which means “to change one’s mind” or “to think differently afterward.” It implies a deep, internal change of mind and heart, particularly regarding sin and one’s relationship to God. Repentance involves:

	•	Acknowledging wrongdoing: Realizing the gravity of sin.
	•	Turning away from sin: It is not merely feeling regret but making a conscious decision to leave sin behind and align with God’s will.

Repentance is about the initial inward change — the shift in one’s attitude and mind towards sin and God. In essence, repentance is an act of the heart and mind that brings awareness of sin, sorrow for it, and a desire to turn away from it.

2. Conversion (epistrephó - Greek: ἐπιστρέφω)

The word “be converted” comes from the Greek word epistrephó, which means “to turn back” or “to return.” It implies an outward action that results from the internal change brought by repentance. Conversion is:

	•	Turning toward God: After repentance, there is a decisive act of turning one’s life toward God and aligning one’s actions with His will.
	•	A complete transformation: It involves a redirection of one’s entire life toward obedience to Christ. Conversion is not just about leaving sin behind but embracing a new way of living in accordance with God’s commandments and purpose.

Key Distinction:

	•	Repentance (metanoeó) is the inward transformation — a change of mind and heart about sin.
	•	Conversion (epistrephó) is the outward result — a turning of one’s entire life to follow God.

How to explain this in the discourse:

When Peter in Acts 3:19 calls the people to “repent and be converted,” he is calling them to both recognize their sin (repent) and take the next step to align their lives completely with God (be converted). Repentance is where the journey starts — with a heart that realizes the need for change. Conversion, however, is the ongoing process that follows, where that inner change is reflected in a new life, a transformed heart, and actions that reflect obedience to God.

You can weave this distinction into the discourse by explaining that repentance is the doorway to conversion, but conversion itself is the lifelong process of living out that change. You could say:

	“Repentance opens our eyes to sin; conversion changes the direction of our feet toward God’s path. Repentance is the first step, but conversion is the daily walk.”

This can tie into Peter’s example, showing that even after his denial and repentance, his conversion was fully realized as he began to walk in his new calling — preaching and strengthening the brethren.
